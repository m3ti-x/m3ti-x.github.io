About the Lab

M3T&I + X Lab

Exploring the substantive and methodological frontiers of Multilingual, Multimodal, and Multiagent Translation and Interpreting

The M³T&I + X Lab is an interdisciplinary and closely connected research team working at the intersection of human and machine translation, applied linguistics, natural language processing, and emerging language technologies.

M³T&I stands for Multilingual, Multimodal, and Multiagent Translation and Interpreting. It conceptualizes translation and interpreting (T&I) as complex, technology-mediated, and socially situated forms of multilingual communication. The framework examines how communication is produced, mediated, evaluated, and received across multiple languages, communicative modes, and human and machine agents.

The “+ X” reflects the openness and evolving scope of the Lab’s research agenda. It represents any substantive, methodological, or emerging dimension that can extend and deepen our understanding of M³T&I. Our research may therefore explore how M³T&I is produced, transmitted, evaluated, received, and experienced in society, drawing on a broad range of approaches, including psychometric, computational, experimental, and mixed methods.

We warmly welcome passionate researchers to join our team.

Research Directions

Evaluation and Reception

Developing and applying human, automated, and human-AI approaches to evaluate translation and interpretation produced by both humans and machines in various contexts, while examining how these outputs are perceived, understood, trusted, and used by diverse audiences.

Intelligent Teaching and Learning

Advancing and reimagining translation and interpreting education through the principled integration of generative artificial intelligence, intelligent assessment, and emerging language technologies into teaching, learning, feedback, and professional training.

Cognitive Modelling

Investigating and modelling the cognitive processes underlying AI-mediated translation, interpreting, and assessment through methods such as eye-tracking, think-aloud protocols, keystroke logging, and other process-tracing techniques.

Meta-research

Synthesizing and critically evaluating research on human and machine translation and interpreting through systematic reviews, meta-analyses, evidence mapping, and other forms of research-on-research.

Publication Highlights

Can large language models evaluate interpreting as reliably as humans?

In this study, we conducted one of the first large-scale examinations of large language models as zero-shot evaluators of spoken-language interpreting. Using 541 pre-scored English–Chinese consecutive and simultaneous interpretations from the Interpreting Quality Evaluation Corpus, we compared GPT-4o and DeepSeek-R1 with trained human raters across three dimensions: scoring reliability, severity, and validity.

Read full highlight

How ready is AI to evaluate human translation and interpreting?

As artificial intelligence becomes increasingly capable of assessing complex language performance, we asked a critical question: How robust, valid, and transparent are current AI-based approaches to evaluating human translation and interpreting?

Read full highlight

What makes an effective rubric for assessing interpreting?

Rubrics are widely used in interpreter education, professional certification, and research, yet their development and effectiveness have rarely been examined systematically. In this study, we conducted the first large-scale systematic review and meta-analysis of rubric-based interpreting assessment, investigating how rubrics are developed, designed, applied, and validated.

Read full highlight

Can adaptive technology make simultaneous interpreting easier to learn?

Simultaneous interpreting places exceptional cognitive and emotional demands on beginners, who must comprehend, reformulate, and produce speech almost concurrently. In this study, we investigated whether computerized dynamic assessment (CDA) could support learners precisely when these processing difficulties arose.

Team

Chao Han
Lab Director
Department of Chinese Studies, National University of Singapore

Dr. Han is an interdisciplinary-minded applied linguist whose work integrates psychometric modeling, cognitive process tracing, and natural language processing to advance manual and automated evaluation and reception of translation and interpreting. Over the years, he has developed a robust and programmatic research agenda, termed Quality Assessment in Multilingual, Multimodal, and Multiagent Translation and Interpreting (QAM³T&I, see Han, 2025). His recent work has been published in leading peer-reviewed journals including Interpreting, Target, Language Testing, Language Assessment Quarterly, and Computer Assisted Language Learning. Dr. Han currently serves as an Associate Editor for Language Testing in Asia (Springer Nature) and sits on the Editorial/Advisory Board of Language Testing (Sage) and Interpreting: International Journal of Research and Practice in Interpreting (John Benjamins).

Join the Lab

We are always happy to hear from motivated PhD and Master’s students from different academic backgrounds who are interested in the evaluation and reception of multilingual, multimodal, and multiagent translation and interpreting.

At the M³T&I + X Lab, you will join a friendly and collaborative research community, receive close mentorship, and have opportunities to connect with wider academic and professional networks.

We also warmly welcome visiting PhD students, academics, and research collaborators from around the world who would like to spend time with us, exchange ideas, or develop joint research in the Department of Chinese Studies at the National University of Singapore.

Interested in joining or working with us? Please send your CV and a short introduction to your research interests to Dr. Chao Han at chaohan@nus.edu.sg. We would be delighted to hear from you.
